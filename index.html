Summary

Tejas is a mechanical engineering graduate with a master's degree in aerospace engineering, specializing in propulsion. His work is primarily focused on numerical modeling of fluid mechanics and combustion, with a strong interest in connecting simulations to physical reality.

He works extensively with CFD across RANS and LES, with exposure to DNS, and places particular emphasis on verification, validation, and model fidelity rather than black-box simulation. Alongside this, he has hands-on experimental experience in laboratory-scale combustion and flow diagnostics, giving him a practical understanding of measurement uncertainty and data quality.

Comfortable with Python, C++, and FORTRAN, he develops efficient post-processing and HPC workflows, and is currently exploring machine-learning-assisted data analysis and CFD to augment traditional numerical approaches.

Numerical Finite Volume Analysis of Incompressible Flows

This project focuses on the numerical solution of canonical partial differential equations relevant to fluid mechanics and heat transfer. Finite Difference and Finite Volume methods were implemented from scratch in Python and FORTRAN to study stability, convergence, and regime-dependent flow behavior.

Governing Equations & Numerical Methods

  • 1D Heat Diffusion Equation - Finite Difference Method (explicit scheme)
  • Linear Advection Equation - Finite Volume Method (upwind discretization)
  • 1D Burgers' Equation - Finite Volume Method (nonlinear convection)

Numerical Analysis

  • Grid convergence and time-step sensitivity studies performed
  • Stability behavior analyzed using CFL-based constraints
  • Results validated against analytical solutions and benchmark cases
  • Comparison of diffusive vs convective dominance across regimes

Numerical Results

Turbulent Combustion Simulation with OpenFOAM

This project investigates turbulent premixed and non-premixed combustion using OpenFOAM-based reacting flow solvers. Canonical counterflow flames and combustor-like configurations were simulated to analyze flame stability, heat release, and pollutant formation under varying operating conditions.

Physical Models & Solvers

  • Premixed combustion - XiFoam (flame wrinkling model)
  • Non-premixed combustion - reactingFoam
  • Turbulence-chemistry interaction modeling
  • Thermal transport and species evolution

Numerical Setup & Workflow

  • Automated mesh generation using Gmsh
  • Bash-based case setup and execution pipelines
  • Improved physical model fidelity by 18-22%
  • Reduced simulation setup time by ~40%

Simulation Results

Thermochemical Modeling with Cantera and Python

This project focuses on zero-dimensional thermochemical modeling of gaseous combustion systems using Cantera and Python. Constant-pressure and constant-volume reactors were simulated to study ignition behavior, species evolution, and thermodynamic property variation for CH4- air and H2- O2 mixtures under varying pressure and equivalence ratio conditions.

Models & Methodology

  • Zero-dimensional constant-pressure and constant-volume reactors
  • Detailed chemical kinetics:
    • GRI-Mech 3.0 (CH4- air)
    • H2- O2 detailed kinetics
  • Transient species and temperature tracking
  • Thermodynamic property extraction from Cantera mechanisms

Results & Analysis

All simulations and post-processing were performed using Cantera and Python, with visualization using Matplotlib.

Key Outcomes

  • Quantified pressure and equivalence ratio effects on flame temperature
  • Demonstrated the impact of chemical mechanism complexity on predictions
  • Analyzed thermodynamic property variation of burnt gases
  • Developed strong intuition for combustion chemistry and ignition behavior

Experience

Research Intern - Master's Thesis

Sorbonne Université

April 2025 - September 2025

During my M.Sc. program in France, I interned at Institut Jean Le Rond d'Alembert within Sorbonne Université in Paris titled "Instability Analysis of Helical Vortices in Turbine Wakes"

This research internship focuses on the numerical and theoretical analysis of helical vortex instabilities in turbine wake flows. The work combines high-fidelity simulations, linear stability theory, and high-performance computing to identify dominant instability mechanisms and characterize their dependence on flow and geometric parameters.

Numerical Methods & Modeling

  • Development and optimization of in-house solvers (HELIX / HELIX-LIN)
  • Baseflow generation and linearization of the Navier-Stokes equations
  • Direct Numerical Simulations (DNS) and linear stability analysis of helical vortices
  • Identification of dominant instability modes and dispersion relations

High-Performance Computing & Optimization

  • FFT-based solver optimization and parallelization, reducing baseflow computation time by 22%
  • Design and execution of scalable HPC workflows for large parametric studies
  • Systematic sweeps over vortex pitch, core radius, and axial velocity
  • Convergence checks and mesh refinement studies to ensure numerical robustness

Post-Processing & Reproducibility

  • Automated post-processing pipelines using Python and MATLAB
  • Mode extraction and growth-rate analysis
  • Construction of dispersion curves and parameter sensitivity maps
  • Emphasis on reproducible, scalable, and research-grade workflows

Research Impact

  • Improved computational efficiency of in-house instability solvers
  • Characterized instability mechanisms governing turbine wake dynamics
  • Bridged high-fidelity simulations with linear stability theory
  • Strengthened expertise in vortex dynamics, flow instabilities, and HPC-based research
